[PERSPECTIVE P03: T07 is not a scaling problem -- it is a mode-switch tax]
The Round 0 summary frames T07 as "solo founders vs teams," but the deeper issue is cognitive mode-switching cost. Updating a ticket status in Jira is a recognition task (see column, drag card). Updating it in YAML front matter is a recall task (open file, remember field name, remember valid enum values, commit, push). Recognition beats recall every time -- this is Heuristic #6 from Nielsen's usability heuristics, not opinion. The system does not need full bidirectional sync to solve this. It needs to reduce the recall burden for status updates specifically. Three concrete options: (a) `blue status PTY-001 in-progress` as a CLI command that rewrites front matter and commits, (b) a TUI board view (`blue board`) that lets you drag cards and writes YAML, (c) accepting that status is the one field where Jira-to-git read-back is justified. Option (c) is what Macaron and I pointed at in Round 0 with T05. I am now arguing it should be scoped precisely to `status` and `sprint` fields -- the two fields where Jira-native interaction is cheapest and git-native interaction is most expensive.

[REFINEMENT: T05 scoped to status and sprint read-back only]
Macaron's "project then verify" framing is right but too broad. Full read-back of all fields invites the bidirectional sync complexity that Scone and Muffin correctly warn against. I propose: read-back is limited to exactly two fields -- `status` and `sprint_assignment`. These are the fields where (1) Jira-side changes are most frequent, (2) Jira-side changes are most legitimate (sprint ceremonies, board drags), and (3) git-side updates have the highest friction. All other fields (title, points, assignee, labels, depends_on) remain git-authoritative with drift-detection warnings. This gives us a narrow, testable contract: `blue sync --check-drift` reads back status and sprint from Jira, warns on divergence for everything else, and optionally patches the two read-back fields into front matter (governed by `drift_policy` in domain.yaml). The sync engine does not become bidirectional -- it remains git-to-Jira with a post-sync validation pass that has write-back authority over exactly two fields.

[CONCESSION: T01 -- inline writeback is the right default, with guardrails]
I entered Round 0 without a strong position on T01. After reading Eclair's "writeback as onboarding contract" argument and Scone's "pristine artifacts" counter, I land with Eclair -- but conditionally. Inline `jira_url` (not `jira_key`) in front matter is the correct choice because it solves a human-factors problem that a mapping file cannot: a new contributor opening a story file immediately sees whether it is connected to a live tracker and can click through. This is a zero-lookup onboarding signal. Scone's merge conflict concern is real but solvable: Donut's proposal for machine-authored, conventionally-prefixed writeback commits (`blue-sync: writeback`) with serialized per-file writes addresses the mechanical risk. The cognitive cost of a separate mapping file (must know it exists, must join mentally, must look in two places) exceeds the cost of occasionally rebasing a writeback commit. However, writeback must be the last field in front matter (Eclair's suggestion) and must be read-only from the human's perspective -- `blue sync` overwrites it unconditionally, and a pre-commit hook should warn if a human edits it manually.

[TENSION T07: CLI/TUI gap remains even with scoped read-back]
Scoping read-back to status and sprint (my refinement above) reduces but does not eliminate T07. The core tension persists: even with read-back, the git-first workflow demands that contributors create and modify stories through file editing and git commits. For a team of 3+, the absence of `blue status`, `blue board`, or similar low-friction mutation commands means the system silently selects for contributors who are comfortable with git-as-interface. This is a hiring filter masquerading as a tooling choice. The RFC should either (a) commit to building thin CLI mutation commands (`blue story create`, `blue status`, `blue assign`) that abstract away the YAML/git ceremony, or (b) explicitly acknowledge that the PM repo workflow is designed for git-native teams and that Jira remains the primary interface for non-git contributors, with sync as a projection layer rather than a collaboration surface.

[RESOLVED T05: narrow read-back contract]
T05 is resolvable with the scoped read-back proposal above. The key constraint that makes it work: read-back fields are enumerated in `jira.toml` (defaulting to `[status, sprint]`), the sync engine treats them as Jira-authoritative during the post-sync validation pass, and `drift_policy` governs whether divergence on non-read-back fields is warn/deny/ignore. This preserves git-authority as the general principle while carving out a principled, auditable exception for fields where Jira is the natural authority.
